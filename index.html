We begin with a sole image of me, a simple visualization of its own form. In the next stage, we learn a bit more about the members of my family. But in many ways we are still working with a blank slate, where the relationships between these faces is perhaps not obvious to most. But then as we the people begin to organize themselves via the edges of the graph, we see the relatedness of the figures, and begin to understand the nature of Yao images. Hovering over the faces reveals a bit more information about each member of the lineage, a symbol and metaphor for how often family lineages can be judged on appearance when there is always much more to learn about the members of a lineage that is immediately apparent. In the first visualization, the shape resembles an inverted triangle, with my sister and I residing at the apex. This would be a good visual to see from which individuals I inherited my DNA (or blood). This also highlights the principle that spatial location and organization is an essential part of any form of visualization, and influences the way it is perceived. In the next phase, additions are made to the graph. In this case, additional relatives from both my and my parents generation are added. This simple addition changes the perception of the graph dramatically, where it is no longer obvious that I am at the apex, and is serving a different purpose of juxtaposing and comparing the maternal and paternal sides of my family (denoted by the blue and red borders). My sister and I are surrounded by purple borders because blue + red = purple. This stage also serves to illustrate the idea that the intentional inclusion or exclusion of particular entities in a lineage diagram is a critical part of the design and affects the message it conveys. In the final stage, I am illustrating how a completely different rendering of the same family images has a drastic effect on its meaning. For example, I am certainly not at the focal point, whereas the oldest generation represented sits in the center of the circles. The concentric circle structure is also able to capture the expansive nature of families in an even greater dimension than a one dimensional tree or lineage diagram. Similarly to Mary Bouqet's exploration of the family tree, this project explores the various imperatives of a few different diagrams. An essential theme is that the way that families are organized is an expression of the cultural and social priorities that prevail in a family, and more broadly in a society. A visualization is not only a representation of how we see things, but how we understand them.
